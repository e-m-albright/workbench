# Customer Discovery

**Philosophy**: Talk about their life, not your idea. Extract facts from the past, not opinions about the future. Listen more than you talk.

> Based on Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test*. The name comes from a simple test: design questions so honest that even your mom can't lie to you about whether your business idea is good.

---

## The Three Rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea** — The moment you pitch, they switch to "polite mode." Keep the conversation about their reality.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics about the future** — "Would you use this?" invites guessing. "When did you last face this?" invites facts.
3. **Talk less, listen more** — Aim for 80/20. Every word you say introduces bias.

---

## The Killer Question

> **"If you could hire somebody today to just sit next to you and do work for you, what would you have them do?"**

This question bypasses feature requests and solution bias. It reveals:
- What work feels like a burden (pain)
- What they'd delegate if cost weren't an issue (value)
- Where their time actually goes vs. where they want it to go (priority)

Follow up with: *"Why that specifically?"* and *"What happens when that doesn't get done?"*

## Question Shortlist

### Understanding the Problem

| Question | What It Reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| "Walk me through the last time you dealt with [problem area]." | Real workflow, actual friction points, current tools |
| "What's the hardest part about [doing this thing]?" | Core pain — in their words, not yours |
| "Why is that hard?" | Root cause vs. symptom |
| "How often does this come up?" | Frequency = severity signal |
| "What happens when it goes wrong?" | Stakes and consequences |

### Understanding Current Solutions

| Question | What It Reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| "What are you doing about it now?" | Existing solutions, workarounds, willingness to act |
| "What tools/services have you tried?" | Competitive landscape from their perspective |
| "What do you love about [current solution]?" | Table stakes you must match |
| "What do you hate about it?" | Gaps you could fill |
| "How much does this cost you — in time, money, or headaches?" | Willingness to pay, magnitude of problem |

### Validating Demand

| Question | What It Reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| "Have you actively looked for a better way to do this?" | Active vs. passive pain |
| "What would a perfect solution look like for you?" | Their mental model (may differ wildly from yours) |
| "If this problem disappeared tomorrow, what would change?" | Impact scope — is it transformative or marginal? |
| "Who else on your team deals with this?" | Market size signal, org dynamics |

### Testing Commitment (After You've Learned Enough)

| Question | What It Reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| "Would you be willing to try a prototype of something that does X?" | Real interest vs. polite enthusiasm |
| "Can you introduce me to someone else who has this problem?" | Social proof, network signal |
| "What would you need to see to take this to your boss?" | Buying process, decision criteria |

## Red Flags in Conversations

| Signal | What It Means |
|--------|---------------|
| "That's a great idea!" | They're being polite. Dig for specifics. |
| "I would definitely use that." | Future tense = hypothetical. Ask what they do *now*. |
| "You should totally build that." | Encouragement ≠ demand. Ask if they'd pay, and how much. |
| "Let me know when it's ready." | Soft no. Push for a concrete commitment (beta signup, intro, deposit). |
| They keep changing the subject. | The problem isn't important to them. |

---

## Green Flags in Conversations

| Signal | What It Means |
|--------|---------------|
| They describe the problem with emotion. | It's real and it hurts. |
| They've already tried to solve it. | Active pain — they're spending time/money. |
| They ask *you* questions about your timeline. | They want a solution. |
| They offer intros to colleagues unprompted. | Problem is shared and recognized. |
| They pull out their wallet / ask about pricing. | Strongest signal of all. |

## Exploration Areas for New Projects

Before building anything, map these five areas through conversations:

### 1. Problem Space

- Who has this problem? (persona, role, industry)
- How painful is it? (frequency × severity)
- How are they solving it today? (workarounds, competitors, manual processes)
- What's the cost of inaction? (money, time, risk)

### 2. Workflow & Context

- Where does this problem sit in their day/week?
- What happens immediately before and after?
- Who else is involved in the process?
- What tools are already in the mix?

### 3. Decision & Buying

- Who decides to adopt a new tool? (user vs. buyer vs. champion)
- What's the budget cycle? (monthly, quarterly, annual)
- What has to be true for them to switch from the current solution?
- What would make them say no?

### 4. Value & Willingness to Pay

- What outcome would make this "worth it"?
- How do they measure success today?
- What's the ROI framing that resonates? (time saved, revenue gained, risk reduced)
- What's an acceptable price range? (anchor against current spend)

### 5. Channel & Reach

- Where do they go for recommendations? (communities, conferences, peers)
- How did they find their current tools?
- What content do they consume? (newsletters, podcasts, forums)
- Who are the influencers in this space?

## Interview Logistics

### Before

- **Prepare 3 big questions** focused on your riskiest assumptions.
- **Set a learning goal**, not a validation goal. You're here to learn, not to confirm.
- **Keep it casual.** Coffee, not a boardroom. Conversation, not a survey.

### During

- **Don't pitch.** If they ask what you're building, deflect until the end: "I'll tell you in a minute — first I want to understand your world."
- **Follow the energy.** When they light up or get frustrated, dig deeper.
- **Get concrete.** "Can you show me?" beats "Can you describe it?"
- **Ask for introductions.** "Who else should I talk to about this?"

### After

- **Write up notes within 30 minutes.** Memory decays fast.
- **Extract facts, not feelings.** Record what they *do* and *did*, not what they *feel*.
- **Update your assumptions.** What changed? What was confirmed? What's still unknown?
- **Decide: talk to more people, or start building?** Usually: talk to more people.

## Anti-Patterns

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Pitching instead of asking | Remove your solution from the conversation entirely |
| Asking "would you use this?" | Ask "how do you handle this today?" |
| Talking to friends instead of customers | Find strangers who match your target persona |
| Accepting compliments as validation | Push for commitments (time, money, intros) |
| Running surveys instead of conversations | Surveys measure what people *say*. Interviews reveal what they *do*. |
| Only talking to enthusiasts | Seek out skeptics — they reveal real objections |
| Stopping after 3 interviews | Pattern recognition needs 10-15+ conversations |

## Quick Reference

```
Before You Build:
├── 1. Define your riskiest assumptions
├── 2. Find 10-15 people who match your target persona
├── 3. Have conversations (not pitches)
├── 4. Extract facts about past behavior
├── 5. Look for patterns across conversations
├── 6. Update assumptions, repeat if needed
└── 7. Build only when patterns are clear and pain is confirmed

Conversation Flow:
├── Open: "Tell me about how you currently handle [area]."
├── Dig: "Walk me through the last time that happened."
├── Pain: "What's the hardest part? Why?"
├── Stakes: "What happens when it goes wrong?"
├── Solutions: "What have you tried? What worked/didn't?"
├── Delegate: "If you could hire someone to just do this, what would they do?"
└── Close: "Who else should I talk to?"
```
